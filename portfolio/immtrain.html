Overview

Support the development of maintenance skills

As a part of a coursework, our team (of four) was provided with an initial goal to develop a highly interactive and informative system for assitance during training. We had to demonstrate how future maintenance workers can benefit from highly interactive Augmented Reality(AR) technology.

Context

WinGD (Winterthur Gas & Diesel Ltd.) approached our department to develope a proof of concept of how the AR technologies can assist their use-case in future. Since WinGD is a leading developer of ship engines, they asked us to build a prototype that lets the user explore the components of a ship-engine valve.

They provided us with the following materials:

  • A physical valve
  • A virtual model of the physical valve (.FBX)
  • Current training materials as PDF documents

Understanding Needs

Defining Goals

After reading through the documents and understanding the procedure surrounding the maintenance of the valve, we came up with three realistic goals. These goals will facilitate as checkpoints for the project and help us to segregate the system into 3 standalone working modules.

1. Exploratory Training
  • Learn about the physical model with the help of “augmented” elements
2. Disassembly & Assembly
  • Follow step-by-step instructions to deconstruct the elements and reconstruct them
3. Interactive Maintenance
  • Get relevant information to check the status of valve parts & repair them

Defining Interactions

Before diving into the specifics of the designing and implementation, it was important to understand what type of interactions fit well for such a use-case. The interactions were dependent on two specifics: Navigation - Which type is relevant for the use-case

  • Physical egocentric navigation
Manipulation - What all the user can do
  • Selection
  • Positioning
  • Scaling
  • Rotation

Designing Solutions

Sketching - User Flows

I usually start the design process with low fidelity sketches. This helps me to iterate through many design alternatives quickly. We used the Generative design approach:

  1. Idea generation: Individually
  2. Capturing ideas: Sketching
  3. Critiquing: Evaluate each other's ideas and proposals
  4. Synthesis: Analyze inputs from team members

Wireframes

I created medium-fidelity wireframes so as to understand information hierarchy and layout. I used elements from Adobe XD's wireframe template so as to quickly visualize our sketches in a more standard way. We made an iteration at this step after conducting an intermediate design evaluation using the interactive wireframes. Some usability mistakes were recognized and better design alternatives were adopted. We realized that the problem cannot be tackled only by designing an app. Certain physical structural changes were also required.


User Interface Design

Once we tested out all usability mistakes, I started designing the final screens in InVision Studio. We followed a hybrid style of material and iOS design guidelines. The color scheme needed to be fresh but also had to align with the existing mensa and the university branding.

MUK Ecosystem - More than just an App

The Upgraded Physical Model

From our intermediate design evaluation during the wireframing phase, we realized that to provide a hassle-free and quick mensa experience an app would prove to be insufficient. We then revisited our observations and realized that a lot can be improved in the physical structure of the mensa.

Metaphors

We observed three major roles that mensa visitors take while entering mensa from the bottom floor:

  1. Card recharging
  2. Balance checking
  3. Deciding what to eat

Hence we decided to separate the areas related to these roles into different spaces so as to avoid collisions. For balance checking, we made use of the subway ticket checking system metaphor. Any frequent visitor can place their RFID card on the reader placed at the entrance while entering the mensa. This allows them to quickly realize if they should recharge their card at the card recharge machines or not. The vertical screens mounted on the pillars(grey boxes) allows the visitors to check the menu up close. This helps visitors with short-sightedness to easily read the menu.


Informed User and Time-saving

We realized an issue which occurred because of the uniformed price for the food sold by weight . Certain food items are sold by weight (per 100g) at the mensa. These sections include Wok, Organic Meal, and Salad bar. The mensa visitors have to take the food without knowing how much they would need to pay until they reached the checkout counter. These food items are weighed at the billing counter and the user is usually shocked at the final price to pay. We tackled this problem by introducing a food weighing system in these sections.

Also, long queues during peak hours were reduced by the introduction of the express checkout system.

Weighing System

The figure shows a low-fidelity paper prototype(1:1 scale) for the weighing system. Care has been taken so that the system also accommodates the non-frequent mensa visitors. People who won't have the mensa app can still get a printed barcode which can later be scanned at the express checkout station.


Express Self-checkout System

One of the bottlenecks in the current system is the checkout process in mensa. This is mostly because the weighing is done at the billing counter and I/O delays caused by the person sitting at the counter. This sketch shows a potential solution to the problem. The system can automatically recognize food items and charge accordingly. The user can instantly pay using the App or other means.

Learnings

From exploring various data elicitation methods to taking a solution approach apart from designing just a smartphone app, I learned a lot. The biggest fear was to get the user requirements correctly. The answer lies in not only listening to what the user says but also observing what they do. I understood what it takes to successfully design a digital ecosystem around an already existing infrastructure. It's a fruitful practice to follow UX design lifecycle process at every checkpoint of the product development. Evaluating intermediate designs potentially saves a lot of time by providing a realistic idea of whether the finished product will solve the problem in reality or not.
